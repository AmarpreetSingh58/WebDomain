"Death sentence" for online luring

Trevor Wilhelm
Updated: April 14, 2012
Share Adjust Comment Print

Orange, VA. — Sitting in a claustrophobic jail room 900 miles from home, Windsor’s Alan Sauve pulled off his glasses, buried his head in his hands and sobbed at the thought of what is ahead.

After he was caught soliciting sex online from a 14-year-old girl in Virginia, the state sentenced him to 110 years in prison. Virginia abolished parole in 1994.

That means the 48-year-old man — who tried to have sex with a girl not much older than his youngest daughter — will die in prison. He’s still breathing, but he knows his life is over.

“No physical, visual or verbal contact and I get the death penalty?” Sauve said through thick safety glass at the Central Regional Virginia Jail. “That’s what I call it, 110 years is a death penalty. This is not a sentence. It’s a death penalty.”

The jail is perched on the edge of a small town called Orange about 75 miles northwest of Richmond, Virginia’s state capital. About 400 rapists, killers, pedophiles and other criminals live there.

Sauve moved in last November after a conviction on 22 counts of soliciting a child under 15 on the Internet for sexual purposes.

He remains there while he appeals his conviction. He’s on this third and last attempt. Sauve initially pleaded not guilty but said he changed his plea out of fear.

“They said if you go to trial they’re going to find you guilty anyway and you’re going to face the maximum per count,” said Sauve, also holding out hope of an unlikely transfer to a Canadian prison.

If he’d been arrested in Windsor, it’s possible he could be a free man right now. In Virginia, 110 years was the minimum.

After only months in jail, Sauve seems to have aged years. He said he’s lost 50 pounds.

He dragged his still chubby frame into the room with a limp, thanks to a deteriorating fused ankle. He has contracted a severe skin rash that blots out a faded black panther tattoo on his right forearm.

His ashen hair is now in a buzz cut. Pallid pudgy cheeks, carpeted in white stubble, frame a stained yellowy moustache. His striped jumpsuit matches the cold grey of the bricks and bars that confine him.

Every day behind those bars is the same. It begins with the first of four head counts.

After that there is breakfast, perhaps some dry waffles, when Sauve is reminded he will spend the rest of his life eating with only plastic spoons.

He lives with 22 other sex offenders in a small room full of bunk beds. The group is separated from the rest of the jail population. It’s for their safety. Even other criminals don’t tolerate sex offenders.

From Sunday to Thursday it’s lights out at 11:30 p.m. Friday and Saturday are “late nights,” when they can stay up until 2 a.m.

“You sleep or you watch TV,” said Sauve. “Nothing else to do.”

There is a lot of time to think. He spends hours poring over court documents and stories about criminals who got lighter sentences, making notes with a bendable pen that can’t be turned into a weapon.

He thinks about his elderly parents, who he lived with before his arrest. He’s also consumed by the thought of losing his daughters, from two different marriages. They are 26 and 13.

“I feel I ruined our last name,” said Sauve.

The younger girl’s mother doesn’t let her communicate with him.

“I always think, has she forgotten about me?” said Sauve. “What does she think of me?”

Thinking about his family, and his life in prison, causes another thought to take hold.

“There have been times it’s gone through my head about suicide,” said Sauve. “I think about that stuff all the time. What stops me is hoping my appeal will go through. If I’m done my appeals, there’s nothing else for me after that.”

During some of his short phone calls — he must call collect at $25 for eight minutes — he asks his parents what they think of him.

“They just said ‘you’ve got to pay for what you did,’” said Sauve. “I don’t think I did anything wrong. What did I do?”

Sauve was arrested Nov. 13, 2010, after he posed as a 16-year-old boy named Dominic to start a relationship with the young Virginia girl. He sent nude pictures of himself. The chat was explicit.

When Sauve threatened to shoot himself, the girl confided in her guidance councillor, who went to police. An officer assumed the girl’s identity.

Sauve was arrested on the Detroit side of the tunnel after unwittingly chatting with the officer for 10 months. He was on his way to meet the girl, get her pregnant and bring her back to Windsor.

“He wanted to meet and not just have sex but have a child, with a child,” said Rusty McGuire, Louisa County’s Commonwealth Attorney.

Despite his guilty plea, Sauve now claims none of that is true.

“I’m not attracted to children,” said Sauve, whose eight-year stint as a school bus driver ended when he went to jail.

On the day he was arrested, Sauve said he was headed to Detroit for gas. He pulled up to a customs booth at the tunnel and handed over his identification.

“All of a sudden there was eight customs officers, all guns drawn,” said Sauve.

He was hauled to the Detroit police station, booked and fingerprinted.

“They said ‘you are a wanted fugitive in west Virginia,’” said Sauve. “I said I’ve never been to Virginia.”

But Sauve told The Star he did chat online with girls from Virginia. He belonged to an adult chat group, where members talk about sex. It was separated by age groups, he said, the lowest of which was 18 to 25.

“I had friends in every room.”

He was in the chat room one day when a message popped up.

“She said she was 18 and lived in Virginia.”

Their online relationship took on a life of its own.

“It just got out of control,” said Sauve. “Just started talking about sex. Role playing.”

Sauve said he eventually stopped the conversations. No particular reason.

“Didn’t feel like talking no more,” said Sauve.

Then one day, there was another message.

“All of a sudden, she started up again,” he said.

But this time, the girl was actually an undercover officer with the Louisa County Sheriff’s Office.

Sauve admits to telling the so-called girl he wanted to get her pregnant, but he never planned to actually do it.

“Just role playing.”

McGuire, who prosecuted Sauve, doesn’t buy that.

“He’s going to offer you 1,000 excuses like they usually do,” said McGuire. “‘Oh I was just experimenting, it was just role playing.’ I’ve done literally 100 of these cases. Pretty much you get the same answers every time. When he tells you that he’s role playing, then say to him why did you show up? That kind of takes away the role playing, doesn’t it?”

The fact Sauve remains unrepentant is proof a long sentence is necessary, McGuire said.

“In Mr. Sauve’s case — with the extreme graphic nature of the offence, with the interest in wanting to impregnate a child — that sort of deviance is really difficult to ever be treated,” he said. “I personally don’t think it can be treated. The only outcome was to convict him of all charges. Do I feel satisfied in this case that he will never harm another child? Yes. And that means I’ve done my job.”

The 110-year sentence, with five years per charge, was the best Sauve could have hoped for. The maximum, with 30 years for the first charge and 40 for each subsequent count, would have been 870 years.

“I don’t understand how they can give me that much time for solicitation when I had no contact with this person,” said Sauve.

Virginia has already set the precedent for harsh sentences never imagined in Canada.

George Spiker, 55, was sentenced in 2010 to 100 years on five counts of soliciting a child over the Internet.

That same year, former pastor Irvin “Pete” Baldwin was convicted on three counts of using the Internet to solicit sex from a minor. The 81-year-old was sentenced to 90 years.

In Canada, the maximum for the worst luring cases is 10 years. It’s rarely if ever given to people with no criminal history, like Sauve.

Windsor’s Michael John Branton, 23, got six months house arrest and three years probation after pleading guilty in November to luring a 13-year-old girl online for sex. At the time of the offence, when he also had cyber sex with her, he was in teacher’s college.

In July, Bruce Kyle from St. Catharines pleaded guilty to luring an 11-year-old Windsor girl. Her mother caught him undressing on a webcam while speaking to the girl over the phone. Kyle also pleaded guilty to possessing and accessing child pornography. He got 15 months.

As it normally goes in Canada, Kyle is serving time for all his charges concurrently. Down south, they do it differently.

“The default position in Virginia is consecutive,” said McGuire.

That’s one reason Sauve was slapped with so much time.

“He did this for so long, he did it so many times and that was the problem,” said McGuire. “The other thing you have to keep in mind is how many other children were there? Do you think you get caught the first time?

“If you know somebody is a school bus driver who is reaching out to have sex with children, how much incarceration would you think they’d need to ensure they won’t do it again?”

It’s an area of the justice system where Canada falls short, said McGuire.

“Exploiting children is an epidemic across this entire world,” he said. “It is a global problem and it is time that we as citizens and public safety officials hold the line. When a predator comes into our courtroom my job is make sure justice is served so they can never prey on a child again.”

McGuire said Canadian lawmakers have fallen behind the rapidly changing times. Twenty years ago, he said, predators could only access kids through babysitters, family members or abducting them from playgrounds.

“Children today are a mouse click away from those who prey on children.” he said. “So you have to make your laws tough, because what do you think the price is for trying to exploit a child and going to meet a child for sex?”

Windsor lawyer Kirk Munroe, who worked in the U.S. as a prosecutor and defence counsel, agreed some dangerous people need to be incarcerated for extremely long periods. But he added they are in the minority.

“The problem I have with that argument is its application,” said Munroe, now a defence lawyer. “You get 100 people in a room and they’ll all argue about who should be put away for the rest of their natural life, and you’ll probably get 100 different opinions. The problem as always is where to draw the line. The problem I have with a lot of the sentencing that goes on down in the States is that it’s very harsh and very punitive.”

Those harsh sentences, he added, are often meted out for political gain. Many judges and prosecutors in the U.S. are elected, including McGuire.

“Judges know that especially on a high-profile case, you get known as a lenient sentencer, you’re going to have someone run against you the next time around,” said Munroe.

Despite that, McGuire maintains that seeing someone sentenced to long prison sentences will stop the next person from committing the crime.

“A lot of problems come from Canada, just like a lot of problems come from the U.S.,” said McGuire. “And I can tell you this: it is a deterrent.”

Munroe isn’t so sure. After 22 years in Florida, which has some of the toughest sentencing in the U.S., he moved here. Rampant crime down south was part of the reason.

Munroe felt he couldn’t let his children play on the street in his upscale Miami neighbourhood. He would tell his wife to watch for people following her home from the grocery store.

“It was getting crazy, and they were sentencing very heavy,” said Munroe. “So the idea that heavy sentences make a safer society is crazy, because it didn’t. It’s much safer here.”
