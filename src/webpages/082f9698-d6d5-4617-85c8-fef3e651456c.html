Bagnall: Nortel's bankruptcy 10 years on — what was lost

James Bagnall
Updated: January 19, 2019
Share Adjust Comment Print

On Jan. 13, 2009, nearly a dozen board members of Nortel Networks gathered in a small office building near Toronto’s international airport. They had already deliberated for days about the wisdom of taking their next, history-altering step.

Their company, Canada’s iconic builder of telecommunications networks, was scheduled two days later to make a $107-million interest payment on its debt (all figures U.S.). Although Nortel had more than $2 billion in cash on hand, much of it was held by foreign subsidiaries or committed to joint ventures.

Such was the state of Nortel’s business in the midst of what became known as the Great Recession that its long-term debt had recently been downgraded to junk.

Nortel chairman Harry Pearce called for a vote on the question none of the directors wanted to entertain: Should Nortel file for court protection from its creditors? Bankruptcy, in other words. One by one, the directors, including former deputy prime minister John Manley, voted “yes.”

Mike Zafirovski, the company’s CEO since 2005, was shocked. He had been expecting something closer to a tie vote. Nortel would file for bankruptcy the next day, Jan. 14, in Canada, the United States and Europe. Although the move would be couched in soft terms, that this was just a tactical step designed to help Nortel restructure its affairs and re-emerge a stronger company, no one had a good feeling about this.

Indeed, just a few months later, the board opted for the fateful strategy of selling off Nortel’s businesses one by one. By 2011, the Nortel brand had disappeared into the operations of Ciena, Ericsson, Avaya and Genband — multinationals that collectively paid $3 billion to acquire Nortel businesses that employed about 4,500 in the Ottawa area. A consortium of six firms led by Apple and BlackBerry shelled out $4.5 billion for Nortel’s treasure trove of patents.

And, with that, the capital region’s largest private-sector employer was gone, along with all that it represented.

In 2019, we can view the events of the past decade somewhat more dispassionately, and assess the impact of this corporate disaster on the community at large.

Somewhat surprisingly, the buyers of Nortel’s businesses retained most of the workers. Collectively, Ciena and the others today employ more than 3,000 in the Ottawa area.

However, this understates the importance of the role Nortel played as a technology flagship — its Ottawa operations handled global R&D for a corporation that sold telecom gear and software in dozens of countries. Nortel was a magnet for technical talent on six continents. In the Ottawa area, the company was an important source of business for hundreds of local suppliers.

Little wonder, then, that the region’s tech sector has struggled so hard to achieve previous heights. Consider that in 2008, tech employment in Ottawa and Gatineau averaged about 58,000. Last year, it was 44,000.

Yet, somewhat surprisingly, the region’s overall economy scarcely suffered, at least in terms of its labour force. Here’s why: during the same 10-year period, the number employed directly by the federal government increased by roughly the same amount, averaging 133,000 in 2018.

The inverse relationship between Nortel and government helped in other ways, too. Although Nortel had vastly overbuilt its real estate in anticipation of a perpetual internet boom, the federal government was in a position to snap up one empty building after another. The apotheosis was its acquisition of Nortel’s glittering R&D campus at 60 Moodie Dr. — now being converted into the headquarters for some 8,500 Department of National Defence staff.

At its peak in 2000, Nortel’s Ottawa-area workforce, including contractors, was about 16,000. Not only was that 60 Moodie Dr. location (then known as 3500 Carling Ave.) completely jammed, Nortel operations were scattered throughout the western part of the region in dozens of facilities.

This gives a better idea of what was really lost.

It’s easy to forget just how long was the path to Nortel’s final destruction — from the near-death experience of the 2001 telecom crash to the 2009 economic recession. It gave the region extra time to adjust to the trauma, and for government real estate experts to justify their purchases of Nortel towers. Entrepreneurs planted seeds. A group of snowboard enthusiasts launched the e-commerce platform that now provides the basis for Shopify’s global business. Pythian, a database company, added hundreds of staff. Mitel began a string of acquisitions.

Also, Nortel sold its assets at such bargain basement prices that the buyers — most notably optical communications specialist Ciena — could afford to take the time necessary to properly integrate the businesses. Ciena’s 1,600 Ottawa-area employees comprise that firm’s single largest centre of operations.

The result is that today, one of Kanata’s biggest political issues is traffic congestion. Not all of it is being generated by high-tech. Cannabis giant Canopy Growth has hired a couple of hundred workers for its Kanata operation. Thousands of federal government employees have moved into Kanata and commute east.

Wherever you go in the capital region, you will find evidence of Nortel’s gene pool, sometimes in surprising places. Robert Wang, the founder and CEO of Instant Brands — maker of the smart appliance everyone knows as Instant Pot — spent four years at Nortel during the late 1990s boom. Former Nortel managers fund startups and guide entrepreneurs through their participation on company boards. Len Anderson, a former Nortel employee, runs a Kanata business that refurbishes aging communications networks, many of them with the Nortel logo.

Even so, neither the alumni or the flourishing of small startups have made up for the losses at Nortel, either in terms of head count or the intangibles associated with having an anchor tenant with a global brand name.

Nearly everyone associated with the company recalls their shock at the news of the 2009 bankruptcy filing. Though there had been forewarnings, Nortel’s sheer scale made it seem too big to fail.

But within the enterprise, there was rot. Not with the accounting — a yearlong criminal trial ending six years ago would determine that top executives did not cook the books. No, the weakness was in the structure of the company and in the vision of those who led it.

Gary Kunis, a former Cisco executive who became Nortel’s chief science officer in 2005, saw the failings most clearly. Kunis calculated that Nortel was No. 1 or 2 in just a handful of its 30 plus businesses. In the world of telecommunications gear, the global lead for wireless technology lay with Ericsson while Cisco was tops in internet gear. Huawei, the Chinese telecom champion, was the emerging leader in low-cost telecom equipment across the board. That left Nortel with its traditional strength in telephone technology and optical transmission gear — and neither of these sectors would post significant growth in the years to come.

Kunis had also been stunned by how antiquated were Nortel’s accounting and supply chain systems. Cisco, an arch-rival, employed internal operations technology so precise that its CEO John Chambers could determine each night what products sold how much nearly everywhere in the world. At Nortel, Kunis discovered the organization’s structure was so complex, business managers had trouble even establishing cost estimates for new products.

Kunis left Nortel after just a few months.

When Zafirovski took over as CEO late in 2005, he tried to put an accounting scandal to bed. Nortel had the year before sacked CEO Frank Dunn and two senior accounting executives for allegedly bending accounting rules to produce executive bonuses. To settle the inevitable class action suits that erupted after the firings, Zafirovski signed off on a deal that paid out $575 million in cash and 62.9 million company shares.

That cash would easily have covered Nortel’s debt interest payments that came due early in 2009. Perhaps it is unfair to focus on this transaction to the exclusion of all the other ways Nortel managed to squander its inheritance. But the class action settlement simultaneously encapsulated two of the firm’s most critical errors — the unwillingness of the 2004 board to push back against the too-quick and flawed audit of the company’s books, and Nortel’s failure to buttress its cash reserves when it had the opportunity.

A third critical weakness was the board’s inability to see that Nortel occupied the high ground in the wrong parts of the telecommunications industry, leaving it fatally exposed when the Great Recession hit.

Ten years ago, all of this sad history was shouldered by a group of company directors who stared straight at the facts as they were. They could do little more than acknowledge Nortel’s days as an independent firm were done.
